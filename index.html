The magnitude and distribution Japanese earthquake (2000-2022)

The data, collected from the USGS website, records historical earthquake data of magnitude 6 or greater that have occurred in Japan since 2000. The data set contains information on the time, location, intensity and magnitude of each major earthquake, as well as the focal depth.


Background About Japan

Japan's is located at the intersection of multiple tectonic plates, coupled with related trenches and active volcanic activity, makes it an earthquake-prone region.

About the earthquake

Magnitude: a scale that quantifies the energy release of an earthquake. It is a logarithmic scale, where an increase of 1.0 indicates roughly 32 times more energy release.

Focal Depth: describes the depth underground where the earthquake rupture begins.

Scale:
Hinged on the magnitude of the earthquake:
Moderate (5.5 - 6.0),
Strong (6.1 - 6.9)
Major (7.0 - 7.9)
Great (8.0 or greater)

Distribution on Map


Earthquakes in Japan occur mainly in the ocean, which is the main reason for the frequent tsunamis in Japan. The most frequent quakes occurred off the eastern coast of Japan, followed by the Kyushu and Ryukyu Islands in the southwest and the Hokkaido region in the north.

The most famous of these is the massive Tohoku earthquake and subsequent tsunami in 2011, which directly led to the nuclear leakage incident and has long troubled the Japanese government and people.


Trend and Frenquency

Hint: Use the brush in the bottom graph to specify the time frame

- The average magnitude of Japan's larger quakes is generally below magnitude 7.
- The average focal depth has been below 50km for quite long time, which means they tend to be more destructive.
- In most years, earthquakes of magnitude 6 or above that occur in Japan have a frequency of less than 5.

From the 2D HeatMap on the left side,
it is obvious that earthquakes with shallow depth and lower magnitude occur more frequently in Japan.

Relationship between Magnitude and Depth

There seems to be no absolute numerical relationship between magnitude and focal depth.

But it is clear that most of the earthquakes with major or great scale are of shallow origin, which means their focal depth are under 70km.

This also shows that earthquakes with shallower focal depth tends to be more destructive.

Earthquakes with deeper focal depths are relatively less destructive because their energy has weakened as it travels to the surface.
